Gender and Climate

Global Trends

  • Global Catastrophe: Climate change is a global catastrophe with far-reaching effects, including increased natural disasters and ecosystem changes.
  • Disproportionate Impact: The effects of climate change do not affect men and women equally; women are disproportionately susceptible due to social, cultural, and economic factors.
  • Climate Disasters: Droughts, floods, storms, and rising sea levels worsen existing inequalities, widening gaps in economic opportunity and resource availability.
  • Financial Losses: Women's financial losses from climate change highlight the need for targeted solutions.
  • Access to Resources: Unequal access to food, land, and water resources is exacerbated by climate change.

  • Important Data: Climate change causes billions of dollars in economic losses annually due to declining natural resources, agricultural failure, and infrastructure damage. According to the World Bank, yearly global economic losses from climate-related disasters have sometimes approached $520 billion.
  • Women in Sensitive Industries: Women, especially in low-income countries, are disproportionately represented in climate-sensitive industries like agriculture and fishing. 43% of agricultural workers worldwide are women, many of whom operate on small, vulnerable farms susceptible to droughts and floods.
  • Inequalities in Work: Women generally have less access to insurance, credit, and financial services compared to men, which hampers their ability to recover from economic shocks caused by climate change.
  • Domestic Burden: During climate disasters, men may migrate to urban areas in search of work, leaving women to handle domestic duties with limited resources, thus exacerbating their financial burden.
  • Overarching Perspective: These financial losses reinforce women's cycles of poverty. Women typically have smaller savings and are less able to recover following disasters due to the gender wage gap.

Macroeconomic Effects:

The macroeconomic implications include decreased labor force productivity and increased gender inequality, negatively impacting the economies of individual countries.

Displacement Caused by Climate Change and Gender Inequalities

  • Synopsis of Displacement: Climate change is a major driver of displacement. The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre (IDMC) estimates that in 2020 alone, climate-related disasters displaced over 30 million people.
  • Impact on Women and Children: Displacement disproportionately affects women and children, particularly in regions with traditional gender roles that limit their mobility (e.g., Central America, Sub-Saharan Africa).
  • Safety Concerns: Displaced women are more vulnerable to gender-based violence, including trafficking, sexual exploitation, and domestic abuse, particularly in refugee camps or makeshift shelters with inadequate security.
  • Health Concerns: Access to maternal and reproductive health services is often limited for displaced women and girls, with restricted access to basic amenities like sanitation and clean water increasing health risks.
  • Education and Employment: Displacement disrupts girls' education, diminishing their future economic opportunities. Women's ability to work is also hindered as they take on caregiving responsibilities and face reduced mobility.

Extended Social Repercussions:

Displacement can entrench cycles of poverty for women and hinder their ability to rebuild their lives. Some areas, such as Pacific Island nations threatened by rising sea levels, may become permanently uninhabitable due to climate change, resulting in lasting relocation.

Impact at the Macro Level:

The millions of women uprooted by climate change exacerbate global social instability, placing additional strain on economies, governments, and humanitarian organizations. The long-term effects of women's institutional marginalization on gender equality are profound.

Resource Access: Limited Water, Land, and Food Resources

  • Depletion of Resources: Climate change leads to resource depletion, including water scarcity, deteriorating soil quality, and reduced agricultural output. These changes significantly impact women, who often manage food production and water collection for their households.
  • Water Scarcity: In many regions, women and girls must travel long distances to fetch water. This burden increases during droughts and water shortages, leading some girls to drop out of school to assist with water collection, perpetuating educational disparities.
  • Food Insecurity: Women represent a significant portion of small-scale farmers in developing countries, yet their access to land, technology, and agricultural resources is limited. Climate-induced crop failures decrease their food production, threatening household food security.
  • Land Rights: Cultural and legal barriers often restrict women's land ownership. Even when women work on the land, they may have no legal claim to it. This lack of land rights hinders their access to government support or foreign aid in the aftermath of climate-related disasters.

Impact at the Macro Level:

Globally, limited access to food, water, and land exacerbates gender inequality. Women's economic opportunities diminish, and their contributions to resource management are undervalued. Resource scarcity can also lead to conflicts, often placing women and girls in precarious situations.

International Efforts in Global Policy Response and Gendered Climate Solutions

  • Global Recognition: The need to address the connection between gender and climate has gained increasing global attention. Initiatives like the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) established the Gender Action Plan (GAP) to integrate gender perspectives into climate policies, while the Paris Agreement includes measures for gender-responsive climate action.
  • Implementation Challenges: However, the implementation of these policies remains inconsistent. In many countries, gender concerns are often sidelined in climate initiatives, revealing a gap between commitment and action.
  • Case Example: Bangladesh exemplifies effective integration of gender in climate resilience planning. Women play a crucial role in disaster risk reduction (DRR) initiatives, being trained to lead their communities and act as first responders during cyclones and floods.

Ongoing Challenges:

Despite these advancements, women remain underrepresented in local and global decision-making processes related to climate change. Women-led climate initiatives often lack adequate funding, and patriarchal structures continue to hinder women from attaining significant leadership roles in climate action.

About Gender Data Gap

  • Importance of Quality Data: To effectively track gender equality development within the context of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), high-quality gender data must be routinely and frequently collected.
  • Obstacles to Accessibility: Evaluations reveal notable obstacles in the accessibility of gender data, with numerous variables unique to a specific gender associated with "emerging statistical areas," where measurement techniques are either in their infancy or lack maturity.
  • Impact of Data Gaps: The absence of official statistics creates significant gaps at the national level, impeding national tracking of SDG development as well as regional and global monitoring.
  • IAEG-SDG Indicators: The Inter-Agency and Expert Group on Sustainable Development Goal Indicators (IAEG-SDGs) currently classifies only 12 of the 54 gender-specific indicators, or 22 percent, as Tier I, indicating sufficient information is available to track development in every location.
  • Global Concern: This discrepancy in data availability affects both rich and developing countries, highlighting a universal concern.
  • Challenges in Data Collection: Constraints such as limited financial and technical support from national statistical systems, uneven data disaggregation, delayed submission of reports to international systems, and poor data distribution complicate the situation.
  • Need for Collaboration: There is often a lack of cooperation between data producers within nations, which hampers the data collection process.
  • Biases in Data: Biases ingrained in definitions, classifications, and survey methodologies compromise the validity and comparability of gender statistics. For instance, labor force surveys that solely inquire about male involvement may overlook the contributions and challenges women face in the workplace.
  • Addressing Challenges: To achieve accurate monitoring and advance gender equality, it is imperative to address these challenges and improve the quality of gender statistics.
